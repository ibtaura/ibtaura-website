Understanding Metabolic Health: Why BMI Tells Only Half the Story

For decades, Body Mass Index has served as the primary screening tool for assessing weight-related health risk. A simple ratio of weight to height, it categorises individuals as underweight, normal, overweight, or obese. But the science of metabolic health has moved far beyond this blunt instrument, revealing a reality that is considerably more nuanced and clinically significant.

BMI was never designed to be a diagnostic tool. Developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s as a statistical tool for studying populations, it was adopted into clinical practice largely because of its simplicity. It requires no blood tests, no imaging, and no specialised equipment. A scale, a measuring tape, and a calculator are sufficient. This convenience made it ubiquitous, but it also masked its fundamental limitations.

The most critical limitation of BMI is that it tells you nothing about metabolic function. Two individuals with identical BMI values can have radically different metabolic profiles. One may have normal blood glucose, healthy insulin sensitivity, optimal lipid levels, and low levels of visceral fat. The other may be insulin resistant, pre-diabetic, have elevated triglycerides, and carry dangerous amounts of visceral adipose tissue around their organs. BMI treats these two individuals as metabolically equivalent. They are not.

1. What Is Metabolic Health?

Metabolic health refers to the efficient functioning of the body's energy processing systems. At its core, it describes how effectively your body converts food into energy, regulates blood sugar, manages lipid metabolism, maintains healthy blood pressure, and controls inflammation. When these systems operate within optimal parameters, the body maintains homeostasis and resists chronic disease. When they break down, the cascade toward metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and other conditions begins.

The clinical definition of metabolic health is typically assessed through five key markers, each with established threshold values. An individual is considered metabolically unhealthy if they meet three or more of the criteria for metabolic syndrome, though emerging evidence suggests that even a single abnormal marker may signal early metabolic dysfunction deserving attention.

Five Criteria for Metabolic Syndrome

Metabolic syndrome is diagnosed when three or more of the five markers exceed their threshold values. Each marker represents a distinct aspect of metabolic dysfunction.

2. Insulin Resistance: The Metabolic Tipping Point

If there is a single mechanism that underlies most metabolic disease, it is insulin resistance. Understanding this process is essential for understanding why metabolic health matters far more than body weight alone.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the beta cells of the pancreas. Its primary role is to facilitate the uptake of glucose from the bloodstream into cells, where it can be used for energy or stored as glycogen. When you eat a meal containing carbohydrates, blood glucose rises, the pancreas releases insulin, and that insulin signals cells throughout the body to absorb glucose. Blood sugar returns to baseline, and the system resets.

Insulin resistance occurs when cells become progressively less responsive to insulin's signal. The muscle cells, liver cells, and fat cells that normally respond to insulin by absorbing glucose begin to require higher and higher concentrations of insulin to achieve the same effect. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin, a state known as hyperinsulinaemia, which can maintain normal blood glucose levels for years.

But this compensation comes at a cost. Chronically elevated insulin promotes fat storage, particularly in visceral adipose tissue. It drives inflammatory signalling pathways. It alters lipid metabolism, raising triglycerides and lowering HDL cholesterol. It increases sodium retention, contributing to elevated blood pressure. And over time, as the pancreas fails to keep pace with escalating demand, fasting blood glucose begins to rise, progressing through pre-diabetes toward frank type 2 diabetes.

Insulin resistance is not a disease that arrives suddenly. It is a continuum that develops over years, often decades, and it can be detected and reversed long before blood glucose ever becomes abnormal. This is where the opportunity for prevention lies.

3. Glucose Variability: Beyond Fasting Blood Sugar

Traditional metabolic assessment relies heavily on fasting blood glucose, a single snapshot taken after an overnight fast. While this metric has clinical value, it captures only one dimension of glucose metabolism. The emerging science of glucose variability reveals that how your blood sugar behaves throughout the day, particularly in response to meals, exercise, stress, and sleep, provides a far richer picture of metabolic function.

Glucose variability refers to the magnitude and frequency of blood sugar fluctuations over time. In a metabolically healthy individual, blood glucose rises modestly after meals, typically peaking at 130 to 140 mg/dL, and returns to baseline within 90 to 120 minutes. The overall range across a day might span just 30 to 40 mg/dL. In someone with early metabolic dysfunction, the same meals may produce spikes exceeding 180 mg/dL, followed by reactive dips below baseline, creating a pattern of wide, erratic swings that the body struggles to manage.

Glucose Response to Different Meal Types

The same caloric intake produces dramatically different glucose responses depending on macronutrient composition. Refined carbohydrates trigger steep spikes and reactive dips, while protein, fat, and fibre produce a gentler, more sustained elevation.

Research has shown that high glucose variability, even when average blood sugar remains within normal range, is associated with increased oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, and accelerated development of cardiovascular disease. The body appears to suffer more damage from wild fluctuations between high and low glucose than from modestly elevated but stable levels. This is why fasting glucose alone can be misleading: it tells you where you started the day, but not what happened during it.

4. Visceral Fat vs Subcutaneous Fat: Location Matters

Not all body fat is created equal. The human body stores fat in two fundamentally different compartments, and the distinction between them has profound implications for metabolic health that BMI completely obscures.

Subcutaneous fat is stored beneath the skin, distributed across the body in familiar patterns. The fat on your arms, thighs, hips, and the layer just beneath the skin of your abdomen is subcutaneous. This type of fat is relatively metabolically benign. It serves as energy storage, insulation, and cushioning. While excess subcutaneous fat contributes to body weight and alters body composition, its direct contribution to metabolic disease is comparatively modest.

Visceral fat is an entirely different entity. Stored deep within the abdominal cavity, surrounding and infiltrating the liver, pancreas, intestines, and kidneys, visceral adipose tissue is metabolically active in ways that subcutaneous fat is not. It functions almost as an endocrine organ, secreting a range of inflammatory cytokines, hormones, and signalling molecules that directly interfere with insulin signalling, promote systemic inflammation, and alter lipid metabolism.

This is why two individuals with identical BMI values can have radically different health trajectories. A person with a BMI of 26 who carries most of their excess weight as subcutaneous fat on their hips and thighs faces a very different risk profile than someone with the same BMI who carries their weight as visceral fat around their organs. The first may be metabolically healthy despite being classified as overweight. The second may be developing insulin resistance, liver disease, and cardiovascular risk despite looking relatively lean.

Waist circumference is a far better predictor of metabolic risk than BMI. A waist measurement exceeding 102 centimetres in men or 88 centimetres in women is associated with significantly elevated risk, regardless of overall body weight.

5. Metabolically Healthy Obesity: A Controversial Concept

The concept of metabolically healthy obesity has generated significant debate within the medical and research communities. It refers to individuals who meet the clinical criteria for obesity based on BMI but who exhibit none of the metabolic abnormalities typically associated with excess body weight. Their fasting glucose is normal. Their insulin sensitivity is preserved. Their blood pressure is healthy. Their lipid profile is optimal. Their inflammatory markers are low.

Estimates suggest that between 10 and 30 percent of individuals classified as obese are metabolically healthy by standard clinical criteria. These individuals challenge the prevailing assumption that excess body weight inevitably leads to metabolic dysfunction and disease.

However, the concept remains contentious for several important reasons. Longitudinal studies have shown that many individuals classified as metabolically healthy obese at baseline transition to metabolically unhealthy status over time. The conversion rate varies across studies but generally ranges from 30 to 50 percent over a follow-up period of five to ten years. This suggests that metabolically healthy obesity may, in many cases, be a transitional state rather than a stable phenotype.

Furthermore, even metabolically healthy obese individuals appear to carry somewhat elevated cardiovascular risk compared to metabolically healthy normal-weight individuals, though the magnitude of this excess risk is substantially smaller than that seen in metabolically unhealthy obesity. The practical implication is that metabolic health markers deserve more clinical attention than body weight alone, but that excess body weight should not be dismissed as irrelevant even when metabolic parameters appear normal.

6. Continuous Glucose Monitoring: A Window into Real-Time Metabolism

Continuous glucose monitoring technology has transformed our ability to observe metabolic function in real time. Originally developed for diabetes management, these small sensors, typically worn on the back of the upper arm, measure interstitial glucose levels every few minutes, producing a continuous data stream that reveals the dynamic behaviour of blood sugar throughout the day.

For individuals without diabetes, CGM data has been revelatory. It has demonstrated that apparently healthy individuals can exhibit glucose spikes exceeding 180 mg/dL after certain meals, experience nocturnal glucose dips that disrupt sleep quality, and show markedly different responses to identical foods depending on factors like meal timing, sleep, stress, and prior exercise.

  1. Personalised dietary guidance. CGM reveals which specific foods cause excessive glucose spikes in a given individual, a response that varies enormously from person to person due to differences in gut microbiome composition, insulin sensitivity, and metabolic efficiency. What spikes one person's glucose dramatically may produce a minimal response in another, making personalised data far more valuable than generic dietary advice.
  2. Meal sequencing and timing insights. CGM data consistently shows that eating fibre and protein before carbohydrates within the same meal can reduce the glycaemic response by 30 to 40 percent. Similarly, consuming the same meal at different times of day produces different glucose responses, with evening meals typically causing higher and more sustained spikes than identical meals eaten earlier.
  3. Exercise timing optimisation. Even a brief walk after eating can substantially flatten the postprandial glucose curve. CGM data allows individuals to see this effect in real time, providing immediate, tangible feedback that reinforces the behaviour.
  4. Sleep and stress quantification. Poor sleep and acute psychological stress both elevate blood glucose through cortisol-mediated pathways. CGM makes these effects visible, transforming abstract concepts like stress management into quantifiable, data-driven priorities.

7. Wearable Metabolic Tracking: The Next Frontier

While dedicated CGM devices have pioneered real-time metabolic monitoring, the future lies in integrating metabolic sensing capabilities into everyday wearable devices. This integration would bring metabolic awareness into the same continuous, passive monitoring framework that already exists for heart rate, activity, and sleep.

Several promising approaches are under active development. Non-invasive glucose sensing using optical spectroscopy, bioimpedance analysis, and electromagnetic detection represents the most actively pursued frontier. While no consumer device has yet achieved the accuracy required for clinical glucose monitoring without a needle-based sensor, the pace of research suggests that viable solutions may emerge within the next several years.

Meanwhile, existing wearable sensors already provide meaningful metabolic insights through indirect markers. Heart rate variability patterns correlate with autonomic function and insulin sensitivity. Skin temperature fluctuations reflect metabolic rate changes. Activity and sleep data contribute to energy expenditure estimation. When these data streams are combined with periodic blood work and dietary information, they create a comprehensive metabolic profile that can be tracked and optimised over time.

By combining continuous physiological monitoring with contextual data about activity, sleep, and behaviour, the platform aims to provide actionable metabolic intelligence that goes far beyond what any single metric, including BMI, can offer. The goal is not to replace clinical metabolic assessment, but to fill the vast gaps between clinic visits with continuous, personalised data that empowers individuals to understand and improve their metabolic health before disease has a chance to establish itself.

The future of metabolic health assessment will not be defined by a single number on a scale or a single fasting blood draw. It will be defined by continuous, multi-dimensional monitoring that captures the full complexity of how your body processes energy, every hour of every day.
