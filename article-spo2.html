Understanding Blood Oxygen Saturation: The Vital Sign on Your Wrist

Every cell in your body depends on a continuous supply of oxygen to function. Blood oxygen saturation, measured as SpO2, tells you how effectively your cardiovascular and respiratory systems are delivering that oxygen. It is one of the most fundamental vital signs in medicine, and it is now available on your wrist.

For decades, pulse oximetry was confined to hospitals, emergency departments, and intensive care units. It was the tool clinicians reached for when a patient's breathing was laboured, when surgery was underway, or when a respiratory condition needed monitoring. The small clip placed on a fingertip could tell a physician, within seconds, whether the blood was carrying enough oxygen to sustain normal function.

The migration of this technology to consumer wearables has been one of the most significant developments in personal health monitoring. Devices like Aura Clarus now measure SpO2 continuously, revealing patterns that a single clinical reading could never capture. Nocturnal dips, exercise recovery curves, altitude responses, and the subtle signatures of respiratory conditions all become visible when oxygen saturation is tracked over time rather than at a single point.

1. What SpO2 Actually Measures

SpO2 stands for peripheral capillary oxygen saturation. It is a percentage that represents the proportion of haemoglobin molecules in the arterial blood that are currently bound to oxygen, relative to the total amount of haemoglobin available. A reading of 98 percent means that 98 out of every 100 haemoglobin molecules passing through the sensor are carrying oxygen.

Haemoglobin is the iron-containing protein within red blood cells responsible for oxygen transport. Each haemoglobin molecule can bind up to four oxygen molecules. When haemoglobin is fully loaded with oxygen, it is called oxyhaemoglobin and appears bright red. When it has released its oxygen to the tissues, it becomes deoxyhaemoglobin and takes on a darker, bluish-red colour. This colour difference is the physical principle that makes pulse oximetry possible.

It is important to understand that SpO2 measures saturation, not the total amount of oxygen in the blood. A person with severe anaemia could have a normal SpO2 reading because the limited haemoglobin they have is fully saturated, even though their total oxygen-carrying capacity is dangerously reduced. SpO2 tells you about the efficiency of oxygen loading at the lungs, not the total oxygen delivery to tissues.

2. The Oxygen-Haemoglobin Relationship

The relationship between oxygen and haemoglobin is not linear. It follows a sigmoidal curve known as the oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve, and understanding this curve is essential for interpreting SpO2 readings correctly.

Oxygen-Haemoglobin Dissociation Curve

The S-shaped curve shows that small drops in oxygen pressure at high saturation levels cause minimal changes in SpO2, but once saturation falls below approximately 90%, even small decreases in oxygen pressure cause rapid desaturation.

At the upper portion of the curve, where SpO2 typically sits for healthy individuals at sea level, the relationship is flat. This means that relatively large changes in the partial pressure of oxygen in the blood produce only small changes in saturation. This is a protective mechanism: the blood remains well-saturated even when oxygen delivery varies modestly.

However, the curve steepens dramatically at lower saturations. Below approximately 90 percent, even small decreases in oxygen availability cause rapid drops in saturation. This is why clinicians treat a reading below 90 percent as a clinical emergency. The steep portion of the curve means that the body's oxygen reserves are nearly exhausted, and further decline can be precipitous.

The difference between 98 percent and 94 percent SpO2 may seem small numerically, but it represents a fundamentally different position on the dissociation curve and a significant reduction in the body's oxygen safety margin.

3. Normal Ranges and What Abnormal Readings Mean

For a healthy individual breathing room air at sea level, normal SpO2 ranges from 95 to 100 percent. Most healthy adults at rest will consistently read between 96 and 99 percent. Readings below 95 percent are considered below normal and warrant attention, while readings below 90 percent indicate hypoxemia and require medical evaluation.

Context matters enormously when interpreting SpO2 readings. A reading of 92 percent in a healthy 30-year-old at sea level is a cause for concern and warrants investigation. The same reading in a patient with severe chronic obstructive pulmonary disease may be their stable baseline, carefully managed with supplemental oxygen therapy. Similarly, a reading of 93 percent in someone who has just ascended to 3,000 metres elevation may reflect normal acclimatisation rather than pathology.

Conditions Associated with Low SpO2

Persistent or recurrent low SpO2 readings can indicate a range of respiratory and cardiovascular conditions. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, heart failure, sleep apnoea, and interstitial lung disease all produce characteristic patterns of desaturation. In many cases, wearable SpO2 monitoring can detect these patterns before they become clinically apparent, particularly when they occur primarily during sleep or physical exertion.

Transient Desaturation Events

Brief drops in SpO2 that resolve spontaneously are common during sleep, particularly during the transition between sleep stages. Mild desaturation events lasting a few seconds are generally normal. However, frequent desaturation events, especially those dropping below 90 percent and lasting more than 10 seconds, can indicate obstructive sleep apnoea, a condition affecting an estimated one billion people worldwide, the majority of whom remain undiagnosed.

4. SpO2 During Sleep: A Window into Respiratory Health

Nocturnal SpO2 monitoring is perhaps the most clinically valuable application of wrist-based pulse oximetry. During sleep, the body's compensatory mechanisms for maintaining oxygen levels are reduced, respiratory drive decreases, and the airway is more vulnerable to obstruction. This makes sleep the period during which oxygen desaturation is most likely to occur and most likely to reveal underlying pathology.

SpO2 Patterns During Sleep: Normal vs Sleep Apnoea

Normal sleep shows stable SpO2 with minor physiological variation. Obstructive sleep apnoea produces a characteristic sawtooth pattern of repeated desaturation and recovery events throughout the night.

In obstructive sleep apnoea, the upper airway collapses repeatedly during sleep, blocking airflow for periods typically lasting 10 to 60 seconds. Each apnoea event produces a measurable drop in SpO2, followed by a recovery as the brain triggers a brief arousal to restore airway patency. This sawtooth pattern, repeated dozens or even hundreds of times per night, is one of the most recognisable signatures in wearable SpO2 data.

The clinical significance of untreated sleep apnoea extends far beyond daytime fatigue. Chronic intermittent hypoxia has been linked to hypertension, atrial fibrillation, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and increased all-cause mortality. The repeated cycles of desaturation and reoxygenation generate oxidative stress and systemic inflammation that damage the cardiovascular system over time.

An estimated 80 percent of moderate to severe obstructive sleep apnoea cases remain undiagnosed. Continuous wrist-based SpO2 monitoring has the potential to identify these individuals years before traditional clinical pathways would detect their condition.

5. Altitude, Exercise, and Environmental Effects on SpO2

SpO2 is not a fixed value. It responds dynamically to environmental conditions, physical activity, and the body's moment-to-moment metabolic demands. Understanding these normal variations is critical for distinguishing physiological responses from pathological changes.

Altitude Effects

As altitude increases, atmospheric pressure decreases, and with it the partial pressure of oxygen in the air. At sea level, the air contains approximately 21 percent oxygen at a total pressure of 760 mmHg. At 2,500 metres, the percentage remains the same, but the reduced atmospheric pressure means each breath delivers significantly less oxygen to the alveoli. SpO2 typically drops by two to four percentage points at this altitude, and can fall below 90 percent at elevations above 4,000 metres in unacclimatised individuals.

The body adapts to altitude through a process called acclimatisation, which involves increasing red blood cell production, enhancing the efficiency of oxygen extraction at the tissue level, and adjusting ventilation patterns. This process takes days to weeks. Monitoring SpO2 during altitude exposure provides objective data on how well an individual is acclimatising and whether they are at risk for altitude-related illness.

Exercise Response

During moderate exercise, SpO2 typically remains stable or drops by only one to two percentage points in healthy individuals. The increased cardiac output and ventilation rate compensate for the increased oxygen demand. However, at very high exercise intensities, particularly in trained endurance athletes, a phenomenon called exercise-induced arterial hypoxemia can occur, with SpO2 dropping to 91 to 93 percent. This is generally not dangerous and resolves immediately upon cessation of exercise, but it can affect performance at the highest levels of competition.

6. How Pulse Oximetry Works: The Technology Behind the Measurement

Pulse oximetry exploits a fundamental optical property of haemoglobin. Oxyhaemoglobin and deoxyhaemoglobin absorb light at different wavelengths. Oxyhaemoglobin absorbs more infrared light and allows more red light to pass through. Deoxyhaemoglobin absorbs more red light and allows more infrared light to pass through.

Traditional fingertip pulse oximeters use transmissive measurement: they shine red and infrared LEDs through the finger and detect the light that passes through on the other side. Wrist-worn devices use reflective photoplethysmography instead, measuring the light that bounces back from the blood vessels beneath the skin. This approach is more susceptible to interference from motion, ambient light, skin pigmentation, and sensor placement, but advances in signal processing have substantially improved its accuracy.

  1. Red LED (660 nm wavelength) — This light is absorbed preferentially by deoxyhaemoglobin. Higher absorption at this wavelength indicates a lower proportion of oxygen-carrying haemoglobin.
  2. Infrared LED (940 nm wavelength) — This light is absorbed preferentially by oxyhaemoglobin. Higher absorption at this wavelength indicates a higher proportion of saturated haemoglobin.
  3. Ratio calculation — The device calculates the ratio of red to infrared light absorption during pulsatile blood flow. This ratio is then mapped to SpO2 using a calibration table derived from human volunteer studies.
  4. Pulsatile component isolation — By focusing on the pulsatile component of the signal, which corresponds to arterial blood flow during each heartbeat, the device isolates the arterial contribution from the background absorption of venous blood, tissue, and bone.

7. Limitations and Factors Affecting Accuracy

While modern pulse oximeters are remarkably reliable, several factors can affect the accuracy of readings, particularly in consumer wearable devices. Understanding these limitations is essential for interpreting your data correctly and knowing when a reading requires clinical confirmation.

Motion artefact is the most common source of error in wrist-based oximetry. Movement creates noise in the optical signal that can be misinterpreted as changes in blood flow. Modern devices employ accelerometer-based motion compensation algorithms to mitigate this, but readings taken during vigorous physical activity remain less reliable than those taken at rest.

Skin pigmentation can also influence readings. Studies have demonstrated that pulse oximeters may slightly overestimate SpO2 in individuals with darker skin tones, particularly at lower saturation levels. This is an active area of research and device improvement, and users should be aware of this potential bias.

Other factors that can affect accuracy include poor sensor contact, extreme cold causing peripheral vasoconstriction, nail polish or artificial nails on fingertip devices, carbon monoxide exposure, and certain intravenous dyes used in medical procedures. None of these factors eliminate the value of wearable SpO2 monitoring, but they underscore the importance of interpreting data within context and seeking clinical confirmation for any concerning readings.

Wearable SpO2 monitoring is a screening tool, not a diagnostic device. Its power lies in detecting trends and patterns over time that warrant clinical investigation, not in replacing clinical-grade measurement for acute medical decisions.

8. The Clinical Future of Continuous SpO2 Monitoring

The transition from spot-check oximetry to continuous, wearable SpO2 monitoring represents a paradigm shift in respiratory and cardiovascular health surveillance. The ability to track oxygen saturation across entire nights, during exercise, at varying altitudes, and through seasonal changes in respiratory health opens clinical possibilities that were unimaginable a decade ago.

For respiratory disease management, continuous SpO2 data can reveal the frequency and severity of desaturation events, track the response to therapy, and provide early warning of exacerbations. For sleep medicine, wrist-based oximetry is emerging as a viable screening tool for obstructive sleep apnoea, potentially reaching millions of undiagnosed individuals who would never undergo formal polysomnography.
